Welcome to A Race athlete

It’s time to level up and get ready for your A race!

araceathlete.com is a place to share and track events, keep an eye on your Watts per Kilograms and see how your week is progressing at a glance.

What is an A race you ask? While you may have other important events, an A race is THE event you plan and train for all year. It is the pinnacle of your season. It is the point of all that suffering.

But we digress, by connecting with your Strava account, you can start tracking your fitness in preparation for your A Race and get insights into how your week is stacking up. Put away those spreadsheets and let us do the tracking.

Activity Search

What does this do?

If you have ever tried to find an activity in Strava, you have probably noticed that you may not get all of the results you expect. This search aims to solve that and deliver insights you probably didn’t even know you wanted.

By default, the page will show your activities in chronological order and you can simply keep pressing search to load more activities.

Results are displayed in three ways.

  • The status bar above the Search button will show the number of activities that are loaded on the page, the number that match the search, and the overall total.
  • The map will show pins for all of the loaded outdoor rides.
  • The list below will show the loaded rides with their stats.

Text Search

Just typing in the search box for a word or words you think you named an activity will probably work. This search uses real words, so “bike” also matches common pluralization like “bikes”. However, if you want more precision, try clicking the Precision box. Text Search also allows you to return your activities sorted by all kinds of metrics. Search defaults to Descending Date, but if you want to sort by biggest Suffer Score, just use the Sort drop down.

Location Search

Ever want to find all the rides you did while on vacation or while visiting the in-laws? Switch over to Location Search by clicking the LOCATION SEARCH tab on the top right. Click anywhere on the map to drop a pin and search from there. Press the location button just above the map to show your current location. By default it will search a 80 km/50 mi radius, but you can change this by typing in a new Max Distance. Click Search and pins will be dropped indicating the loaded results on the page. Location results are limited to the first one hundred entries.

Note: A Race athlete does not store your current location; this information is only used locally on your device. If you choose not to enable browser location, we will use the City from your Strava profile as your location.

Filters

Filters work for both Text and Location searches. Click the “filters” toggle to open the filter panel on the right. At the top you can make commutes and trainer rides Exclusive or Excluded. Default is inclusive.

Below the check boxes are the range filters. Range filters will only include results within the range you specify. You can specify dates, distance, elevation, and a host of other ranges. If you want to know all the rides between certain dates, for instance while you were riding the Tour de France last year, just set the date ranges for those dates and search will return those three weeks. Perhaps you want to know all of the rides you’ve done longer than 100 miles? Move the left distance slider to 100 and search and it will return all rides over 100 miles.

Note: Trainer rides - Currently Zwift and other Virtual Ride software do not match Trainer because Strava has reclassified them as Virtual Rides and doesn’t turn on the trainer option for the activity. Look for an update to fix this in A Race athlete in the near future.

Weekly Stats

What does this do?

Weekly stats are based on metrics collected during a week’s worth of Strava activities. This gives you a quick way to see how your week is shaping up, as well as compare different metrics to each other. Touch any of the charts to show the value for that day.

Not all metrics are available to all users. A Strava Premium account is required for advanced analytics such as Power and Heart Rate. To find out more about that, visit Strava here. A Power Meter and Heart Rate sensor is also required for their respective data.

What does this mean?

Basic Metrics

Distance, Time and Elevation are available to all Strava users.

TSS and Kilojoules - Power Meter

TSS, short for Training Stress Score, also know as Training Load in a Strava Activity or Training Impulse on the Strava Fitness and Freshness chart (here), is a number based on power produced and your current FTP. That looks a bit like this:

// ET: Elapsed Moving Time
// WaW: Weighted Average Watts
// FTP: FTP
((ET * WaW * (WaW / FTP)) / FTP * 3600)

FTP: A Race athlete uses your Strava FTP (here). Keep this up to date for accurate statistics. Originally FTP was based on the amount of power an athlete could produce over an hour. Strava provides an estimate on the Strava Power Curve chart (here ). If you have a few sufficiently long, hard outdoor rides, this number is usually close enough as a starting point. For More on FTP see:

  • TrainingPeaks: What Is Functional Threshold Power?
  • Global Cycling Network (video): How To Do A Functional Threshold Power (FTP) Test
  • TrainerRoad: 20 Minute VS. 8 Minute FTP Test

Kilojoules is the power produced over the whole of the activity. It is closely linked with actual Calories expended.

Suffer Score and Calories - Heart Rate

Strava uses your Maximum Heart Rate and your ride data to determine a Suffer Score. You can set your Maximum on Strava here. It is dependent on your Maximum being correct and you being in good health. Calories are based on your heart rate over the length of the activity and are not as precise as Kilojoules for measuring energy expenditure. That said, if you don’t have a power meter, Heart Rate is the next best thing.

How can I make use of this?

One way to make use of this weekly data is to set weekly TSS or Suffer Score goals. Mind you, not all TSS or Suffering is created equal, but it can help if you are mixing indoor interval sessions and outdoor rides.

Power trumps Heart Rate, so use TSS if available.

Click on the stars next to the titles to “Favorite” events you plan to do and then filter them using the All, Star, or Owner buttons in the main menu.

Power at Altitude

Power at Altitude is an estimation of your FTP at various altitudes so you can better regulate your power expenditure away from home. The chart is based on your City and Country in Strava (here) and current FTP (here) using the Bassett (1) formulas. Because FTP is a Premium Strava feature, the Power at Altitude customized chart is only available to Strava Premium users at this time. This may change in the future.

How can I use this? The measure of a Watt does not change with altitude, so it is easy to redline yourself because you try to match your normal power or become disheartened because your legs do not meet your expectations. Either way, it will set you up for a harder race than needed. What if the number you train to is in sync with the altitude?

Most cycling computers now have Power/FTP. Set up your primary display to show Power/FTP instead of Watts and train using this. Power Zones typically look like this:

*This table is available by choosing an FTP in the Power at Altitude chart.*

Your FTP should be right in the middle of Zone 4. This is why it is important to assess your FTP.

Now when you change altitude, check the Power at Altitude chart and adjust your head unit FTP accordingly and Power/FTP now takes altitude into account. Now you can go out and ride to the same numbers you do at home without the headgames.

Feature Requirements

Some features in A Race athlete require specific data in order for that feature to be useful. If the data is not available, you will see a Feature Requirements notice.

For example, we can’t show you a Power to Weight history graph if your data contains no FTP or Weight data.

Here are some of the checks we do and how to resolve them:

  • A Race athlete Strava Club membership - Anything that requires us to have a history of your Activities requires a club membership. Club members are allowed unlimited Activity storage while non-club members are limited to 21 activities per visit. You can join the club (here).

  • To be a Strava Premium member - Much of the advanced analytics data is only available to Strava Premium members, for example, Power and Heart Rate data. Upgrade your Strava account to Premium for this feature (here).

  • An entry for FTP on Strava - The feature requires you have a current FTP on Strava (here).

  • An entry for Weight on Strava - The feature requires you have a current Maximum Heart Rate on Strava (here).

  • An entry for City and Country on Strava - The feature requires at least a general idea about your location for calculating your elevation or time zone here.
